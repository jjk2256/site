LumenBeat 2025

I imagined LumenBeat as a re-interpreted oracle—an object you touch, not speak to, and one that answers with light. We decided to make the heartbeat tangible, which meant printing the form at a grand scale and wrestling with the challenges that came with it. The result is a tactile, breathing object where design and computation turn physiology into something quietly poetic.

LumenBeat is a touch-responsive digital automaton that translates biometric input into shifting ambient light. Designed to nest comfortably against the hand, the object renders a user's heartbeat as expanding and contracting illumination, turning internal physiological rhythms into a spatial, perceptual experience.

The exterior shell—designed to mimic the dynamic rhythm of a handprint through its curvature—was digitally generated in Rhino + Grasshopper and prototyped using two fabrication workflows at New Bedford Robotics Research (NBRR): a large-format 3D printer and a robotic arm with an extruder. Because the form contains hundreds of perforations, the large-format print produced the most consistent and structurally accurate results and became the final body. The hand-touch region required metal-forming using a UR3. It is shaped to mimic a small human-hand to cradle the heart-rate sensor and maintains reliable skin contact.

The system operates through an input → process → output sequence following the project's trigger logic. Input: the user places their hand on the metal-formed surface, activating the heart-rate sensor. Process: pulse data flows to an Arduino-based microcontroller, which interprets the signal, stabilizes fluctuations, and maps the heartbeat onto timing curves. Output: a vertical LED structure animates in synchronized pulses, visualizing the heartbeat as a breathing column of light.

LumenBeat integrates parametric design, hybrid fabrication (3D printing + robotic metal forming), physical computing, and biometric sensing to evoke physiological, and spatial experience into a single interactive object.

Role
Concept and interaction design, physical computation using Arduino, wiring, sensor integration design, lighting design, video, and drawings

Collaborators
NBRR, Adnan Kasubhai, Kitae Kim
